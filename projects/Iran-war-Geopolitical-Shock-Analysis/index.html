Part 0: Rationale for Project

As part of the interview process for a company, i was tasked to identify large cap stocks from the Russell 1000 (market cap >USD 30 Billion) that have outperformed the S&P500. This analysis focused on comparing the outperformance for two phases of 2026, 1. Prewar - 31th December 2025 to 27th February 2026 2. Post-war 27th February 2026 to 10th April 2026. From this data, i had to draw out some conclusions and observations.

I did quite abit of data cleaning and data visualisation for this exercise, so i figured that i turn it into a project with risk attribution analysis for the first part, exploring the data and how the sectors and the companies did in each “market regime” (if any), and take it as a practice in data visualisation. The idea was that the Iran War was an exogenous macro event and we could use it as a stress-test for large-cap equities.

And to expand the project a little, i figured it would be a good place to practice some quant models with Regime Detection and some Factor Modelling which would fit well with this project given its dual time period analysis. Explored abit of everything, going for breadth as part of an exploration into quant models and how they work, and plan to create more projects that delve into each of these models a little more in-depth in the future. I do this in Part 2, where i try to do more complex analysis and bring in data from other sources like WRDS for analysis.

Part 1: Market Microstructure & Risk Attribution

Deconstructing the Benchmark during the Q1 2026 Geopolitical Shock.

For this part, i got the data from Yfinance instead of for WRDS because I was under time-pressure, and got the constituents from the iShares proxy of the Russell 1000, which i figured would at least contain all the major players.

Instead of simply filtering the index by market capitalization, this analysis stress-tests large-cap equities during an exogenous macro event. By peeling back the layers of the benchmark, we can expose the hidden rotational dynamics and severe risk fractures that occurred during the crash.

Section 1: The Benchmark vs. The Breadth

Masking the Rotational Damage Beneath the Surface

Purpose & Implications: The purpose of this analysis is to compare the headline performance of the S&P 500 index against the actual underlying stock breadth and composition. The results demonstrate that the index was artificially propped up by a concentrated handful of names, completely masking the rotational damage happening beneath the surface. The implication is severe: passive benchmark tracking hid extraordinary levels of idiosyncratic risk from investors during the shock.

Section 3: The Efficiency Frontier

The Failure of Passive Beta in a Crisis

Purpose & Implications: This interactive Sortino matrix is designed to evaluate risk-adjusted performance across the universe of individual stocks. By mapping Maximum Drawdown against the Sortino Ratio, the scatter plot reveals that passive index investing (Beta) offered terrible risk-adjusted returns during the shock. This mathematically proves the necessity of active stock picking—isolating true Alpha was the only way to avoid the high-drawdown/low-reward quadrant.

Section 5: Regime Shift Dynamics

Tracing Capital Flight in Real-Time

Purpose & Implications: This visual maps the exact timing and magnitude of capital reallocation before and after the February 27th shock. Using a “ghost layer” baseline, the results expose how pre-shock market leaders were violently liquidated, while defensive safe-havens caught aggressive bids. The implication is that market participants did not just de-risk; they actively rotated, requiring quantitative models to dynamically adjust to new leadership regimes instantly.

def add_subplot_traces(fig, start_date, end_date, highlight_sectors, row, col, add_vline=False):
    s_returns = sector_daily.loc[start_date:end_date]
    sp_returns = sp500_returns.loc[start_date:end_date]
    
    # Calculate Indexed growth (Reset to 1.0 at the start of each slice)
    s_indexed = (1 + s_returns.fillna(0)).cumprod()
    s_indexed = s_indexed / s_indexed.iloc[0]
    
    sp_indexed = (1 + sp_returns.fillna(0)).cumprod()
    sp_indexed = sp_indexed / sp_indexed.iloc[0]
    
    # --- STEP 1: THE GHOST LAYER (Background) ---
    # We plot EVERY sector in grey so they stay visible when highlights are toggled
    for sector in s_indexed.columns:
        fig.add_trace(go.Scatter(
            x=s_indexed.index, y=s_indexed[sector], mode='lines', 
            line=dict(color='lightgrey', width=1.1), 
            opacity=0.25, 
            name=f"{sector}_bg", showlegend=False, hoverinfo='skip'
        ), row=row, col=col)
            
    # --- STEP 2: THE HIGHLIGHT LAYER (Foreground) ---
    for sector in highlight_sectors:
        fig.add_trace(go.Scatter(
            x=s_indexed.index, y=s_indexed[sector], mode='lines', 
            line=dict(color=master_palette.get(sector, 'black'), width=2.5),
