  6. How do courting and marriage alter from one generation to the next?
    1. The evolution of courtship and marriage across the generations highlights the changing social landscape and the family’s shifting circumstances.

  • First Generation (Sunja/Yangjin): For Yangjin, marriage was arranged, a practical union within her own community. For Sunja, it’s a product of crisis. Her relationship with Hansu is a passionate but illicit affair, not a courtship. Her marriage to Isak is a pragmatic act of salvation, born of compassion and necessity rather than romance. Marriage is fundamentally about survival and honor.

  • Second Generation (Noa and Mozasu): This generation faces the challenge of interracial relationships. Noa’s relationship with Akiko, a Japanese woman, is intellectual and secretive, but ultimately doomed by the immense social pressure and his own internalized shame about his identity. Mozasu has a long-term relationship with Yumi, who is also Japanese, and they have a child, but the social stigma of his Korean heritage and his pachinko business always looms. For this generation, love and marriage are fraught with the external barrier of racism.

  • Third Generation (Solomon): Solomon’s relationships are more modern and globalized. His relationship with Phoebe, a Korean-American, seems promising but fails due to a cultural chasm. She cannot comprehend the weight of his family’s history in Japan, and he struggles with his own identity. His complicated connection with Hana, a Japanese girl, is rooted in a shared childhood but is destructive. For Solomon, the options for partnership are wider, but the legacy of his family’s past and his own identity crisis make true connection difficult.

  1. Compare the ways in which the women of this novel—from Sunja to Hana—experience sex. Sex is often a catalyst for major life changes and is rarely depicted as simple or purely pleasurable for the women.

  • Sunja: Her first sexual experience with Hansu is a moment of seeming agency and desire, but it leads to her pregnancy and threatens her with social ruin. It’s a transformative act that sets the entire course of her life. Her sexual relationship with Isak is one of duty and tenderness, which grows into a form of love, but it begins as part of their marriage arrangement.

  • Kyunghee: Her experience is defined by absence. Her infertility is a source of quiet sorrow and shame in a society where a woman’s primary value is often tied to childbearing.

  • Hana: Hana represents a complete rebellion against traditional norms. She uses her sexuality for power, for money (as a hostess and through compensated dating), and for self-destruction. For her, sex is a tool and a weapon, often disconnected from emotional intimacy and ultimately linked to her illness and early death. Her experience is a tragic commentary on the limited, often transactional, roles available to some women.

  1. How much agency and power do you think Sunja really has over her life? Sunja’s agency is a complex question. While she is often a victim of circumstance—her unplanned pregnancy, the poverty she endures, the discrimination she faces—she consistently makes active choices within her limited options.

  • Moments of Agency: She chooses to be with Hansu. She chooses to accept Isak’s proposal rather than become Hansu’s mistress. She decides to start selling kimchi to ensure her family’s survival. She chooses to protect her family by keeping Noa’s parentage a secret.

  • Constraints on Power: Her power is constrained by her gender, her poverty, and her ethnicity as a Korean in Japan. She cannot control the larger historical forces (war, colonialism) or the deep-seated societal prejudice that shape her world.

Ultimately, Sunja’s power lies not in controlling her destiny, but in her resilience and her ability to endure and adapt. Her agency is found in the small, crucial decisions she makes to protect her family and maintain her dignity in a world designed to strip her of both. 1. Sunja tells Noa that “Blood doesn’t matter.” Do you agree? What parts of the novel support or weaken Sunja’s claim?

This is one of the central philosophical questions of the novel.

  • Support for the claim: Sunja’s statement is an expression of her deepest wish and a reflection of her own life. She loves Noa as her own, and Isak was a true father to him in every way that mattered. Mozasu, who is not related by blood to Isak, honors him as his father. The family bond between Sunja and Kyunghee is stronger than many blood relationships. These instances suggest that love, loyalty, and shared experience are what truly constitute a family.

  • Weakening the claim: The entire novel tragically demonstrates that, in the context of their society, blood does matter. Noa’s life is destroyed when he learns of his “tainted” bloodline to Hansu, a yakuza. He cannot escape this biological fact, and it leads to his suicide. The family’s Korean blood dictates where they can live, what jobs they can hold, and how they are treated by society. Solomon’s career is stymied by his Korean blood. The story relentlessly shows that while Sunja’s sentiment is noble, the world they live in is built on punishing people for their blood.

  1. Yangjin and Kyunghee agree that “A woman’s lot is to suffer.” Do you think the women suffer more than the men in this book? If so, in what ways? How does the suffering of Sunja and Kyunghee compare to that of Yoseb? Noa and Mozasu’s?

It’s not a competition of suffering, but the nature of their suffering is different, shaped by gender roles.

  • Women’s Suffering: The women endure a constant, grinding suffering. It is social (ostracism), emotional (grief, shame), and physical (endless labor, childbirth). Sunja carries the shame of her pregnancy and the weight of her family’s survival. Kyunghee bears the sorrow of infertility and the pain of watching her husband, Yoseb, decline. Their suffering is often private and endured with a quiet fortitude.

  • Men’s Suffering: The men’s suffering is often more public and tied to failures of honor, identity, and providing. Isak suffers from illness and imprisonment for his faith. Yoseb is crippled physically and psychologically by the Nagasaki bomb, his pride shattered. Noa’s suffering is a profound identity crisis that he cannot bear, leading to a dramatic, final act. Mozasu suffers from bullying and the social stigma of his profession.

  1. Much is made of Sunja’s fading beauty, as well as the physical appearance of all the women who surround her. What does this reveal about society at this time? Do you see this emphasis on female beauty reflected in present-day culture?
  2. Throughout the book, characters often must choose between survival and tradition or morality. Can you think of any examples that embody this tension?
  3. Many of the main characters struggle with shame throughout their lives, whether due to their ethnicity, family, life choices, or other factors. How does shame drive both their successes and failures?
  4. The terms “good Korean” and “good Japanese” are used many times throughout the book. What does it mean to be a “good Korean”? A “good Japanese”?
  5. “Both men had made money from chance and fear and loneliness.” Pachinko begins with the family of a humble fisherman that, through the generations—and through times of poverty, violence, and extreme discrimination—gains wealth and success. What were the ways in which the family managed to not only survive, but also eventually thrive? What is the relationship among money, race, power, and class?
  6. “Wherever he went, the news of his mother’s death preceded him, wrapping the child in a kind of protective cloud; teachers and mothers of his friends were watchful on his behalf.” In what other ways does death act as a “protective cloud” in this novel?

The quote refers to how Isak’s death protects a young Mozasu from bullying. Death continues to have this strange, protective quality.

  • Isak’s Legacy: Isak’s death solidifies him as a saintly, martyred figure. His memory becomes a guiding moral compass for the family, “protecting” them with a standard of kindness and integrity.

  • Noa’s Suicide: In a tragic way, Noa’s death “protects” him from a life of perpetual shame. It also acts as a dark cloud that hangs over the family, a protective warning to Solomon about the dangers of losing one’s identity.

  • Hana’s Death: Hana’s long, slow death from AIDS forces Solomon to confront difficult truths about life, love, and responsibility. Her death shatters his corporate ambitions and “protects” him from a potentially empty life, forcing him back toward his family and his roots

  1. Compare the many parent-child relationships in the novel. How do they differ across families and generations? What hopes and dreams does each parent hold for their children—and are these hopes rewarded?
  2. Even in death or physical absence, the presence of many characters lingers on throughout the book. How does this affect your reading experience? How would the book have been different if it were confined to one character’s perspective?
  3. Why do you think the author chose Pachinko for the title?
  4. The title works on both a literal and a powerful metaphorical level.

  • Literal: The pachinko business is the literal engine of the family’s financial ascent. It’s the controversial industry that lifts them from poverty to wealth.

  • Metaphorical: A pachinko game is a metaphor for the lives of the characters, particularly Koreans in Japan. The game is largely one of chance, where the player has minimal control. The steel balls are shot into the machine and cascade down, bounced around by pins and obstacles. This mirrors how the characters are buffeted by huge, impersonal forces—history, war, colonialism, systemic discrimination. The game is also known to be rigged; the house always has the advantage. This reflects a society where the odds are stacked against them. Despite the rigged nature of the game, people continue to play, hoping for a jackpot. This symbolizes the family’s unyielding resilience and hope in the face of impossible odds.
