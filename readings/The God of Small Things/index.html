Analysis

Phonetic Deconstruction & Neologisms

Roy often breaks words apart or capitalizes them strangely to show how the twins hear them. This emphasizes their innocence—they hear the sound before they understand the social meaning. - Examples: “Locusts Stand I” (Locus Standi), “Prer NUN sea ayshun” (Pronunciation), or “Thang God” (Thank God). - Function: This technique creates an “insider language” between the twins. It isolates them from the adults and highlights their vulnerability. It also satirizes the English-obsessed, Anglophile culture of their family (like Baby Kochamma), who value “correct” English over human connection.

Reading Backwards

Rahel and Estha often read signs backwards (e.g., sisoD for Diagnosis). - Analysis: This suggests a desire to reverse time or undo events. If you can read backwards, perhaps you can un-live the tragedy. It also reflects their dissociation; they are observing reality from a detached, skewed angle.

Prolepsis (Foreshadowing/Spiraling Time)

The novel is not linear. It starts at the end (the funeral) and spirals inward toward the traumatic event (Sophie Mol’s death and Velutha’s murder). - The “Little” vs. “Big” Events: Roy constantly hints at the tragedy (“in a plausible way,” “it was a small thing”). - Effect: This creates a sense of inevitable doom. As a reader, you are not asking what happens, but how and why. It forces you to focus on the social structures (The Love Laws) rather than just the plot twists.

Synesthesia and Visceral Imagery

Roy describes abstract feelings using physical, often rotting, imagery. - Examples: The “Green” of the swamp, the “smell of old roses,” “blood-red,” or the “yellow church.” - Function: The setting (Kerala) is not just a backdrop; it is a living, breathing, and sometimes malevolent force that swallows the characters.

II. Major Themes

The “Love Laws”

This is the central thesis of the book. Roy writes that these are the laws that dictate “who should be loved, and how. And how much.” - Forbidden Love: The tragedy stems from breaking these laws. Ammu (a high-caste Syrian Christian) loves Velutha (a Paravan/Untouchable). - Parental Love: The failure of parental love is stark. Mammachi loves Chacko blindly but abuses Ammu. Ammu loves the twins fiercely but hurts them in her grief (“I love you a little less”).

The God of Small Things vs. The God of Big Things

  • Velutha is the God of Small Things. He controls the minute, beautiful moments of the children’s lives (fixing toys, holding hands). He represents humanity, touch, and the immediate present.
  • The Big God represents the vast, crushing social machines: The Caste System, The Police, History, and The Family Reputation. The tragedy is that the “Big Things” invariably crush the “Small Things.”

History as a House

Roy metaphorically treats History not as a timeline, but as a physical house that traps people. - Chacko’s Metaphor: He tells the twins that they are trapped outside the “History House,” unable to understand their ancestors because of colonialism (Anglophilia). - Literal Manifestation: The actual History House across the river is where the children hide, where Velutha is beaten, and where innocence ends. It is where the past physically destroys the present.

III. Key Motifs

Motif Symbolism & Meaning
The Moth (Papachi’s Moth) Represents fear, silence, and unexpressed resentment. It lands on Rahel’s heart, symbolizing the chill of lovelessness and the haunting presence of the past.
The River (Meenachal) The boundary between worlds. It separates the “Touchables” from the “Untouchables” (Velutha’s hut). It is the source of life but also the site of death (Sophie Mol’s drowning).
Pickles and Preserves The family factory, “Paradise Pickles & Preserves,” symbolizes the attempt to “preserve” memories and the past. However, you cannot pickle time; it inevitably rots (like the banana jam), just as the family rots from the inside.
The Time (10:00) The narrative frequently references the time or the stopping of watches. It emphasizes that for Estha and Rahel, time effectively stopped at the moment of the tragedy.

On Capitalization (Thoughts and Things)

You are spot on. That capitalization is one of the most brilliant psychological devices in the book. > “The air was full of Thoughts and Things to Say.”

By capitalizing these abstract concepts, Roy does two things: 1. Child’s Perspective: To a child, a “Thought” that you aren’t allowed to say feels as big and physical as a table or a chair. It occupies space in the room. 2. The Weight of the Unsaid: It turns “silence” into a tangible object. The car isn’t empty; it is crowded with these invisible, capitalized “Things.” It validates the children’s anxiety—they aren’t just imagining the tension; the tension is a physical presence sitting next to them.

Defining “The Small Things”

  • The Big Things: These are the structures that govern life. Politics, Caste, Religion, History, The Police, Money, Social Reputation, and “The Love Laws.” These things are rigid, ancient, and often cruel. They don’t care about individual feelings.
  • The Small Things: These are the moments of life. The sensory details (the smell of rain, the texture of a spider), personal intimacy, secrets between children, sexual desire, a touch, a laugh. These are the things that make life worth living.

The Central Tragedy: The novel argues that The Big Things (Society/History) always crush The Small Things (Individual Happiness). Velutha is the “God of Small Things” because he focuses on the immediate joy of the children and Ammu—he fixes toys, he plays, he loves. But because he has no power over the “Big Things” (he is a Paravan/Untouchable), the Big Things (the Police/Caste System) destroy him.

Close Reading: The Drive Back from the Airport

The Context: The family has just picked up Chacko’s ex-wife (Margaret) and daughter (Sophie Mol). The car is packed. The air is thick with tension. Ammu is the outcast; Chacko is desperate to impress; Baby Kochamma is envious.

The Quote (and variations of it): > “Big things were not spoken, only small things.” (Paraphrased essence of the scene). Roy writes: “In the car, nobody said anything… The air was full of Thoughts and Things to Say. But at times like these, only Small Things are ever said. Big Things lurk unsaid inside.”

Analysis of the Scene: A. The Suffocation of Politeness (Mianzi) This scene perfectly illustrates the “silence” you mentioned earlier. The “Big Things” in the car are screaming to be addressed: - Ammu’s humiliation at being treated like a servant. - The racial tension of the white ex-wife returning. - The jealousy the twins feel toward Sophie Mol (the “Loved” child).

However, if anyone spoke of these “Big Things,” the family unit would explode. So, they retreat to Small Things as a survival mechanism. They talk about the traffic, the heat, the luggage, or the car’s engine.

B. “Small Things” as a Distraction vs. “Small Things” as Truth Here is the irony: - For Velutha, “Small Things” are truth (love, play, connection). - For the Family in the Car, “Small Things” are lies (trivial chitchat used to cover up the ugly truth).

C. The Link to the Tragedy This scene foreshadows the end. - Because the family cares more about appearance (a Big Thing) than honesty (a Small Thing), they are unable to protect their own children. - Sophie Mol creates a stir because she is a “Big Thing”—she represents Englishness, whiteness, and success. The twins are treated as “Small Things”—insignificant and interchangeable. - The quote implies that ignoring the Big Things doesn’t make them go away. They “lurk inside.” This lurking energy eventually manifests as the betrayal of Velutha.

Synthesis: Velutha’s Role

You asked: Does the novel suggest that “Small Things” are weak?

Yes and No. 1. Socially Weak: Yes. In the face of the “Big God” (The Police/History), the “Small God” (Velutha) is powerless. A toy boat cannot stop a tank. Love cannot stop a lathi charge. 2. Existentially Superior: No. Roy suggests that the Small Things are the only things that matter. - The “Big Things” (History/Caste) are portrayed as hollow, rotting, and cruel. - The “Small Things” (Ammu and Velutha’s love making, the children’s games) are the only moments of genuine beauty and life in the book.

The Verdict: The novel is a tragedy because it shows that in our world, the “Big Things” usually win the battle, but the “Small Things” are the only reason to fight. Velutha dies, but the memory of him—the Small Things he left behind in Rahel and Estha—is the only thing that survives the wreckage of time.

Velutha as the “God”

In this scene, we finally see why he is the God of Small Things. He doesn’t offer Ammu safety, money, or a future (Big Things). He offers her: - A ridge of muscle on his back. - The taste of the river on his skin. - Being seen.

He creates a sanctuary of “Small Things” where Ammu can briefly exist without the weight of her ancestors or her failures.

The Final Chapter (The Love Scene)

This is the most crucial chapter to understand the book’s message.

1. The Structure: Why end here? The chronological end of the story is depressing: Ammu is dead, Velutha is dead, Estha is silent, Rahel is empty. By ending the book with the beginning of the affair (Ammu and Velutha’s first night), Roy refuses to let “Death” be the last word. She lets “Love” be the last word.
