This is What Inequality Looks Like

★★★★★

Singaporean
Book
Author

Teo You Yenn

Published

February 27, 2025

Summary and the Core Premise of the Book

An ethnographic examination of poverty and systemic inequality in Singapore, challenging the traditional national narrative of meritocracy by revealing the hidden, structural barriers faced by low-income families.

Why I Read This Book

Was a bestseller and wanted to gain an insight from a social, rather than the usual economic, perspective as to the systemic frictions and the hidden costs that are affecting social mobility in Singapore

Key Takeaways

  1. The Relativity of Value: Material goods hold different psychological and practical utility across socioeconomic classes; applying a uniform, middle-class gauge to welfare policy often harms the very people it intends to help.
  2. The “Poor Tax” (Time Poverty): A lack of financial capital necessitates the expenditure of a more finite resource—time. Inefficient daily logistics create a compounding cycle that traps individuals in poverty.
  3. Spatial Inequality: Poverty drastically shrinks an individual’s physical geography and strips away unseen privileges, such as domestic privacy for children.
  4. The Paternalism of Aid: Welfare systems are often built on flawed “myths” regarding the decision-making capabilities of low-income individuals, prioritizing donor preferences over recipient autonomy and dignity.

On the Relativity of Needs and Policy Blind Spots

One of the most profound shifts in perspective the book offers is how the mental and practical value of an object changes depending on socioeconomic status. To a middle-class policymaker, a television might be classified as a “luxury” good because their weekends are subsidized by external enrichment classes, dining out, and paid entertainment. To a low-income household, however, the television is an absolute essential—it is the primary, cost-effective source of family bonding and entertainment. When policymakers apply uniform, middle-class assumptions to define “needs” versus “wants,” they inadvertently design interventions that are fundamentally disconnected from the reality of the people they are trying to assist.

Time Poverty and the “Poor Tax”

Inequality is often measured in dollars, but its most punitive toll is exacted in hours. The book highlights the reality of the “poor tax”, the phenomenon where lacking money means you must spend time instead. Because low-income individuals lack access to premium, time-saving services, they are forced to navigate deeply inefficient systems: relying entirely on slower public transport, queuing at banks and post offices, or spending hours commuting to access basic administrative infrastructure. This creates a vicious cycle; the heavy administrative burden of simply existing in poverty drains the exact temporal and cognitive resources required to break out of it.

Spatial Geography and the Erosion of Privacy

Poverty dictates not just what you can buy, but where you can exist. The spatial reality for the poor is often confined to highly functional, transactional spaces: the childcare center, the bank, the post office, and the market. Furthermore, this lack of spatial capital bleeds into the home, severely compromising privacy, particularly for children. Without the luxury of physical space to retreat, study, or develop autonomously, the environment becomes another structural headwind against upward mobility, reinforcing the physical and psychological boundaries of their socioeconomic class.

The Paternalism of Aid: “Why Must Poor People Always Eat Oats?”

This point is not strictly in this book, but in an article online, that i have linked above in the “Other Reading” section. The article exposes a pervasive, underlying bias in how aid is distributed: the assumption that poverty is a symptom of poor individual decision-making. This manifests in the preference for giving food rations (like oats) or restricted vouchers rather than direct cash transfers. The donor assumes the recipient lacks the capacity to make “good” choices. This paternalistic approach creates a sharp divide, casting the aid giver as superior and the recipient as a subject needing to be steered. As Teo highlights through a resident’s frustration over being forced to eat water-logged oats, this framework ignores the recipient’s autonomy and fails to recognize that they understand their own needs better than the system does. But becuase the system has the power to make decisions on behalf of the recipient, this misalignment of incentives and misconception of “what is best” leads to poor outcomes for the parties involved.

The Erosion of Dignity and the Burden of Proof

Dignity, as the book notes, is like clean air: you only notice its absence when it is in short supply. While professionals (like the author herself) are granted immediate societal respect, low-income individuals are frequently met with skepticism. To access social safety nets, they are required to provide extensive, repeated proof of their poverty. While designed to prevent abuse, these administrative hurdles exact a heavy psychological toll, systematically diminishing the individual and stripping away their dignity just to secure basic survival resources.

National Myths and the Alienation of the Poor

Teo argues that national narratives, such as the “self-reliant Singaporean”, are powerful myths. They are not outright lies, but they have universalizing tendencies that flatten empirical realities and obscure systemic friction. By framing poverty strictly as an individual “mindset” problem, or dismissing it as a mere negative externality of national economic development, the system alienates those left behind. The poor are cast as a tiny, marginal minority experiencing a temporary plight they should simply “work hard” to escape, ignoring the structural barriers that keep the cycle in motion.
